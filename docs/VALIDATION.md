# Validation: SeaVigil vs an authoritative IUU list

> Generated by `scripts/validate_iuu.py`. Re-run to refresh.

**Ground truth:** [CCAMLR Non-Contracting Party IUU Vessel List (2025/26)](https://www.ccamlr.org/en/compliance/non-contracting-party-iuu-vessel-list) - an independent CCAMLR determination, not derived from any SeaVigil signal.

**Question:** applied to vessels an authority has *already confirmed* as IUU, does SeaVigil's data layer resolve them, and does its authorization grading agree?

## Result

- **8 of 18** CCAMLR-listed IUU vessels resolve in the GFW vessel registry that SeaVigil's authorization layer queries.
- **5** are tagged **`IUU`** outright in GFW's registry - an independent second source agreeing with CCAMLR.
- **8 of 8** resolved vessels have **no current fishing authorization** on record, so SeaVigil grades them *unauthorized* - exactly the high-severity flag the listing implies.
- The **10** that do not resolve predate or evade AIS; they are the dark fleet SeaVigil's SAR layer targets, not a miss of the AIS layer.

This closes the loop: SeaVigil's *foreign -> graded authorization* logic, run against vessels confirmed IUU by an independent authority, returns *unauthorized*, and GFW's registry corroborates the CCAMLR listing.

## Honest limits

- This validates the **authorization grading and identity layer**, not the apparent-fishing classifier (whose held-out metrics are in [MODEL_CARD.md](MODEL_CARD.md)).
- GFW authorizations come from RFMO records; an empty record means *no public record*, not proof. National EEZ licences are not public.
- CCAMLR's list is the Antarctic toothfish fishery; many entries are old vessels with limited AIS history, which is why some do not resolve.
